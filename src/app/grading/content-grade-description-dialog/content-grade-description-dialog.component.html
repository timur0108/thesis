Grading of Theses

This part of the guidelines includes the principles of grading theses and explains the values of grades.

Components of the Grade

Graduation theses are graded on the basis of the following four criteria:

1. Content

  • Topicality, actuality, academic value, authoritativeness and comprehensiveness of primary sources;
  • The level of analysis and synthesis of literature;
  • Validity and comprehensiveness of the method(s) used;
  • Logicality of the approach to the topic;
  • Reasoned and comprehensive comparison of the results achieved with previous results;
  • Applicability of the results;
  • Novelty of the results;
  • Quality of the created software.

2. Level of Complexity

  • Compliance of the thesis to the requirements established for bachelor’s and master’s theses;
  • Complexity of novel (scientific) results, viewpoints, created software and approach presented in the thesis;
  • The volume of work done.

3. Appearance

  • Academic, correct and clear use of language;
  • Good technical realisation (clear and logical structure; quality of tables, figures and other illustrative material);
  • Accuracy of references;
  • Cohesion of the text.

4. Presentation

  • The structure and consistency of the presentation;
  • The clearness of the presentation and use of examples;
  • The correct use of language;
  • The capability of answering questions and participating in discussion.

A reviewer makes an evaluation suggestion regarding the first three criteria according to the grading scale presented in Section 5.2.

A defence committee evaluates, besides the first three criteria, the author's presentation and considers the evaluation of a reviewer.

Grading Scale

Each criterion is graded individually as follows:

  • 5 – Very good
  • 4 – Good
  • 3 – Barely acceptable
  • 2 – Poor
  • 1 – Very poor

Explanation of the Scale

As a general rule, only the first four grades should be used. Decimal points can be used in positive grades.

  • Grade 5 (very good) – The work is almost flawless according to the given criterion and is outstanding in some aspects.
  • Grade 4 (good) – The thesis is a good work without major shortcomings.
  • Grade 3 (barely acceptable) – Meets minimal requirements but contains substantial shortcomings.
  • Grade 2 (poor) – Does not meet minimum requirements. Grade 2 in two criteria automatically means F (fail).
  • Grade 1 (very poor) – Used only in exceptional cases (e.g., plagiarism). Automatically results in F (fail).

Final Grade

The suggested final grade is derived from (sum of criterion points) × 5 according to the following scale:

  • A – 91–100 Excellent
  • B – 81–90 Very good
  • C – 71–80 Good
  • D – 61–70 Satisfactory
  • E – 51–60 Pass
  • F – 50 or less Fail
